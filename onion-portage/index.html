Onion Portage

A Data Exploration of Its Layers

By Macilee MacKenzie

May 8, 2025

Onion Portage, also known as Paatitaaq in the Iñupiaq language meaning wild onions, is an area located off the shore of the Kobuk River in Northwestern Alaska. Onion Portage has been a fruitful site for the Iñupiat people through thousands of years as a place for camping, fishing, caribou hunting, and making tools, amongst other activites. It is also a site of great archaeological importance, and for this article, I will be focusing on the archaeological work of Dr. James Louis (J.L.) Giddings and Dr. Douglas D. Anderson there.

Map of Northwest Alaska.

In 1940, Dr. Giddings was conducting research and surveying along the Kobuk River, including excavating at Onion Portage. But it wasn’t until he came back in 1961, that Onion Portage was realized to be a stratified site. During this time, Gidding had excavated a little more than one meter deep until he hit permafrost. More excavations took place two years later in 1963, where they expanded upon their previous work, now that the ground had thawed. Giddings had unexpectedly passed away in 1964, and his student, Douglas Anderson, took over the project. Annually from 1964 to 1968, systematic excavations took place, which were the first of its kind in interior Northwestern Alaska. In the following years until 1973, Anderson periodically conducted excavations at Onion Portage.

Onion Portage Excavation Site, July 1965.

What makes Onion Portage particularly valuable, in an archaeological sense, is that it is a stratified site, meaning that there is distinct layering of sediment. Stratified sites are rarely found by archaeologists in Arctic regions because there is usually very little soil build up over time. Typically, with Arctic archaeology, thousands of years of living is encapsulated within a thin layer of sod. Yet, at Onion Portage it is almost 5 meters (16.4 feet) deep of stratified layers of soil.

Aerial view of Onion Portage along Kobuk River, August 1964.

Paatitaaq (wild onions) nearby the excavation site.

Close-up of the stratigraphy.

The data collected from Onion Portage was similarly very valuable as it could be used to help gain an understanding of how people lived and adapted over time. Due to varying environmental conditions, a single layer can represent a range of different time periods–day, weeks, decades, centuries. These layers are referred to as levels at Onion Portage, and related levels are grouped into a larger category, bands. With Onion Portage being a stratified site, it allowed for an analysis of the different civilizations/people by seeing how they used the site over time.
